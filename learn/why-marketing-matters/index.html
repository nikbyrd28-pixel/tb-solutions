Why marketing matters — old-school vs. new

For generations, businesses in Chester County grew one way: do good work, and people talk. A plumber in Downingtown got recommended at church. A bakery in Kennett Square got mentioned at the ballfield. A sign on Gay Street in West Chester was marketing.

Here's the good news: that still works. Word of mouth is still the most trusted recommendation there is. The problem is what happens right after the recommendation.

What changed: the "check first" habit

When someone hears about your business today — from a friend, a Facebook group, a lawn sign — they almost never call right away. They look you up first. They type your name into Google. They glance at your reviews, your photos, your website.

In that moment, one of two things happens:

  • They find a clean, modern page that answers their questions and makes it easy to reach you → they call.
  • They find nothing, or something that looks abandoned → they quietly call the next business instead. You never even know you lost them.
The simple truth: new marketing isn't about replacing word of mouth. It's about not fumbling the handoff. Old-school gets you the mention — your online presence closes it.

Old-school vs. new, side by side

  • Then: the Yellow Pages. Now: Google search and Google Maps — showing up when someone near Exton or Malvern searches "plumber near me."
  • Then: a newspaper ad. Now: a Google or Facebook ad shown only to people in your towns, only when they're looking.
  • Then: "ask around." Now: reviews — strangers vouching for you in public, 24/7.
  • Then: a storefront window. Now: your website — open at midnight, on every phone in the county.

Why this matters extra in Chester County

Our towns still run on trust and familiarity — that's the charm of West Chester, Downingtown, Kennett Square, and the boroughs along the Main Line. But that cuts both ways: when a local business looks current and cared for online, it inherits all that hometown trust instantly. When it looks neglected, people assume the business is too — fair or not.

And because many local competitors still have outdated sites or no site at all, the bar to stand out here is lower than almost anywhere. A sharp website and a clean Google listing can make a one-person shop look like the most established option in town.

The takeaway

You don't need to "do marketing" in some corporate sense. You need three simple things: look good when people check you out, show up when people search, and make it dead easy to contact you. That's it. Everything else is bonus.
